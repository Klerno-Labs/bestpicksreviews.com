Why You Need a Hammock with a Net


Camping in the wilderness offers a unique escape from the noise of modern life, but it often comes with unwanted guests—mosquitoes, gnats, and no-see-ums. While a standard hammock offers excellent comfort and portability, it provides zero protection against flying insects that can ruin a peaceful night’s rest. If you are searching for the best camping hammock with mosquito net, you are looking for a blend of shelter, sleep quality, and packability. These specialized hammocks feature integrated or attachable netting that keeps bugs out while maintaining airflow, ensuring you wake up refreshed rather than covered in bites. Whether you are backpacking through the Amazon or camping in your backyard, investing in a quality bug net hammock is essential for a comfortable outdoor experience.

Buyer’s Guide

When shopping for the best camping hammock with mosquito net, several technical factors determine whether a product is a good fit for your specific outdoor style. Understanding these elements will help you avoid overpaying for features you won't use or, worse, choosing a hammock that fails in the elements.

Mosquito Net Material and Mesh Size


The primary purpose of a bug net is to create a barrier between you and flying insects. The quality of the netting is determined by two factors: mesh size and material durability.
  • Mesh Size: Most high-quality hammocks use 19x19 inch mesh, which stops larger mosquitoes but lets in small "no-see-ums." If you camp in areas dense with no-see-ums (like the deep woods or coastal areas), look for hammocks with 21x21 inch mesh or specifically mention "No-See-Um" netting. The smaller the holes, the less airflow you get, so there is a trade-off between bite protection and ventilation.
  • Material: Look for high-quality nylon that is treated with waterproofing. It should be lightweight enough to hang with minimal tension but strong enough to resist snagging on tree bark.

Rainfly Durability and Storm Protection


A mosquito net alone is not enough for three-season camping. You need a rainfly to protect the net from rain and UV damage.
  • Hydrostatic Head (HH): This measurement indicates how much water pressure the fabric can withstand before leaking. A rating of 1500mm to 3000mm is standard for backpacking hammocks. The higher the number, the better the storm protection.
  • Design: Look for a "catenary cut" rainfly that utilizes a curved edge rather than straight lines. This design adapts to the sag of the hammock, eliminating sagging fabric that can cause water pooling during heavy downpours.

Suspension System Compatibility


Hammock suspension is where most manufacturers save money, and it is often sold separately. A good suspension system distributes weight evenly and protects the tree bark.
  • Whoopie Slings: The gold standard for ultralight hikers. They are simple to adjust and lightweight.
  • Tree Straps: The safest option for trees, as they distribute weight over a wider area and prevent damage.
