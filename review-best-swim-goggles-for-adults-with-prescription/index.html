Buyer's Guide

To make an informed decision, it is essential to understand the technical specifications and features that differentiate the best swim goggles for adults with prescription from standard swimwear.

Diopter Range


The diopter range determines what prescription strengths the goggles can accommodate. For nearsighted (myopic) swimmers, you will look for a negative number (e.g., -2.0, -4.0). For farsighted (hyperopic) swimmers, you need a positive number (e.g., +2.0, +4.0).
  • Low to Moderate Range (-1.0 to -4.0): Best for mild nearsightedness.
  • Moderate Range (-4.5 to -7.0): Common for many adult swimmers.
  • Strong Range (-7.5 to -10.0+): Required for severe myopia.
  • Custom Options: Brands like PROGEAR offer custom lenses that go up to -20 diopters, which is necessary for those with extreme prescriptions that standard goggles cannot handle.

Prescription Type (Nearsighted vs. Farsighted)


Prescription swim goggles come in two primary categories:
  • Nearsighted (Myopia): These are marked with negative numbers (e.g., -2.00). They are designed to help you see things far away clearly underwater.
  • Farsighted (Hyperopia): These are marked with positive numbers (e.g., +2.00). These are crucial for older adults or those with naturally farsightedness who struggle to read the pace clock or the bottom of the pool wall.

Astigmatism Correction


Standard "off-the-shelf" goggles typically offer only "sphere" correction (powers for near or far vision). They do not correct "cylinder" power, which is used for astigmatism (irregular curvature of the cornea). If you have astigmatism, you may experience blurry or distorted vision in standard goggles. Custom prescription goggles (like the PROGEAR H2O) are designed to handle astigmatism and prism corrections, providing a sharp, undistorted image in all directions.

Lens Type and Tint


The lens tint affects contrast and glare.
  • Mirrored Lenses: These reflect light away from the eyes, reducing glare significantly. They are perfect for bright outdoor environments or highly reflective indoor pools.
  • Clear Lenses: These provide natural light transmission. They are ideal for low-light environments or night swimming.
  • Smoke Tint: Offers a medium reduction in brightness and glare, suitable for overcast days or indoor pools with harsh lighting.

Fit and Peripheral Vision


A good fit prevents leaks and ensures comfort during long sessions.
  • Seal Material: Look for silicone seals. They are hypoallergenic, durable, and comfortable against the skin.
  • Peripheral Vision: If you swim in open water or crowded pools, wide peripheral vision is critical for safety. Models like the Sporti Pro II are engineered with extra-wide sightlines to minimize blind spots.

UV Protection


UV protection is often overlooked but is vital for outdoor swimmers and triathletes. Look for goggles that block 99% or 100% of UVA and UVB rays. High-quality polycarbonate lenses naturally provide this protection, shielding your eyes from harmful sun exposure that can lead to long-term eye damage.

Materials and Durability


The material of the frame and lenses dictates how long the goggles last. Polycarbonate is the industry standard for lenses because it is shatter-resistant and lightweight. For the frame, a flexible but durable plastic ensures the goggles won't snap if you bump into a pool wall. Latex-free silicone is the preferred material for the gaskets to prevent allergic reactions and skin irritation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What diopter range do prescription swim goggles typically cover?
Most standard prescription swim goggles cover a diopter range from -1.5 to -8.0 for nearsighted swimmers and +2.0 to +5.0 for farsighted swimmers. Brands like PROGEAR offer custom solutions that can handle extreme prescriptions up to -20 diopters or beyond.

Are prescription swim goggles covered by insurance?
Yes, high-end custom prescription goggles, particularly those that serve as a medical necessity or can be prescribed by an optometrist, may be covered by vision insurance. You will typically need a written prescription from your doctor to make this claim.

Can I get astigmatism correction in swim goggles?
Off-the-shelf goggles usually only correct for sphere power (nearsightedness or farsightedness) and do not correct astigmatism. For accurate astigmatism correction, you need custom-fitted goggles that allow for cylinder power adjustments.

What is the difference between nearsighted and farsighted swim goggles?
Nearsighted goggles are designed for negative diopters (-1.0, -4.0), helping you see the pool wall clearly. Farsighted goggles are designed for positive diopters (+1.0, +3.0), which help you read the pace clock and see the bottom of the pool clearly, which is often challenging for those with hyperopia.

Are mirrored lenses better for swimming?
Mirrored lenses are excellent for reducing glare and reflection, which can be distracting and eye-straining. They are particularly beneficial in bright, sunny conditions or when swimming in outdoor settings. For indoor swimming, clear or lightly tinted lenses are usually sufficient.

How do I know if swim goggles fit correctly?
A properly fitted goggle should seal snugly against your skin without creating pressure marks. When you open your eyes underwater, you should not feel water leaking in. Additionally, the goggles should stay in place while you move your head side to side without moving the lenses significantly.

Do prescription swim goggles fog up?
Like any eyewear, prescription swim goggles can fog up over time, especially if the anti-fog coating wears off. To prevent this, never touch the inside of the lenses with your fingers and apply a specialized anti-fog spray or solution before swimming.
